# Dog Nutrition

- Adult dogs generally need two meals per day; puppies need three to four.
- Portion size depends on weight, age, and activity level — always follow feeding guidelines on the food label as a starting point.
- Fresh water must be available at all times; dehydration can become serious within hours.
- Protein should be the first ingredient on any quality dry kibble label (chicken, beef, salmon, etc.).
- Dogs are omnivores and thrive on a balanced mix of protein, carbohydrates, fat, vitamins, and minerals.
- Grain-free diets have been linked to dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) in some breeds — consult a vet before switching.
- Human foods toxic to dogs include grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, xylitol (in sugar-free products), chocolate, and macadamia nuts.
- Treats should make up no more than 10% of a dog's daily caloric intake to avoid weight gain.
- Senior dogs (7+ years for most breeds) often benefit from food formulated for lower calorie density and joint support.
- Puppies require food labeled "for all life stages" or "puppy" — adult food lacks the nutrients needed for growth.
- Overweight dogs are at higher risk for joint disease, diabetes, and shorter lifespan; rib visibility check is a simple home gauge.
- Switching food brands should be done gradually over 7–10 days to prevent digestive upset.
