# Dog Behavior

- Dogs are social pack animals and require daily human interaction to stay mentally healthy.
- Puppies have a critical socialization window from 3–14 weeks — exposure to varied people, sounds, and environments during this time reduces fear responses later.
- Excessive barking is usually caused by boredom, anxiety, attention-seeking, or territorial response — not stubbornness.
- Destructive chewing in adult dogs is most often a sign of under-stimulation or separation anxiety, not spite.
- A wagging tail does not always mean a happy dog; tail height and speed together indicate emotional state (low slow wag = uncertainty, stiff rapid wag = arousal).
- Positive reinforcement (reward-based) training produces faster learning and fewer fear responses than punishment-based methods.
- Dogs communicate stress through yawning, lip licking, whale eye (showing whites), and turning away — these are calming signals, not defiance.
- Most dogs need at least 30–60 minutes of physical exercise daily; working breeds may need 2+ hours.
- Mental enrichment (puzzle feeders, sniff walks, training sessions) is as important as physical exercise for behavioral health.
- Separation anxiety signs include destructive behavior, excessive vocalization, or house-soiling that only occurs when the owner is absent.
- Resource guarding (food, toys, space) is normal dog behavior but should be addressed with a trainer before it escalates.
- Dogs thrive on routine — consistent feeding, walk, and sleep schedules reduce anxiety significantly.
