# Cat Nutrition

- Cats are obligate carnivores — they require nutrients found only in animal tissue (taurine, arachidonic acid, vitamin A) and cannot thrive on plant-based diets.
- Adult cats typically do well with two meals per day; free-feeding dry food can contribute to obesity.
- Wet food is strongly recommended as a primary or supplemental food source because cats have a low thirst drive and are prone to chronic dehydration.
- Taurine deficiency in cats causes heart disease (dilated cardiomyopathy) and blindness — always feed food labeled complete and balanced.
- Fresh water should always be available; many cats prefer moving water and drink more from pet fountains.
- Human foods toxic to cats include onions, garlic, grapes, raisins, chocolate, xylitol, alcohol, and raw dough.
- Cats cannot taste sweetness and do not benefit from carbohydrate-heavy diets; high-carb dry kibble can contribute to diabetes over time.
- Kittens need kitten-specific food until 12 months — adult food is deficient in calories and nutrients for growth.
- Senior cats (11+ years) often lose muscle mass; higher-protein diets formulated for seniors can help maintain lean body condition.
- Sudden food changes can cause digestive upset or food refusal; transition over 7–10 days by mixing old and new food.
- Overweight cats are at significantly elevated risk for diabetes, hepatic lipidosis (if food is withheld), and joint disease.
- Fish-flavored foods are fine occasionally but should not be a cat's sole diet — high mercury content and can cause thiamine deficiency over time.
