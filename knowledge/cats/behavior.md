# Cat Behavior

- Cats are solitary hunters by nature and are more independent than dogs, but still require daily social interaction and environmental enrichment.
- Kneading ("making biscuits") is a comfort behavior rooted in kittenhood nursing — it signals contentment and trust.
- Slow blinking at a cat is a feline signal of relaxation and non-threat; cats often slow-blink back, indicating trust.
- Scratching is a normal, necessary behavior for claw maintenance, stretching, and scent marking — provide appropriate scratching posts to protect furniture.
- Hiding is a common stress or illness response in cats; a cat that suddenly hides more than usual warrants attention.
- Cats are crepuscular — most active at dawn and dusk — which is why many engage in "zoomies" at night.
- Litter box issues (urinating outside the box) are often a response to a dirty box, wrong box size, wrong litter type, or medical causes.
- A cat's tail position communicates mood: upright = confident/happy, low = scared/submissive, puffed = threatened, wrapped around body = calm.
- Cats communicate through chirping, trilling, chattering (at birds), and meowing — meowing is primarily directed at humans, not other cats.
- Multi-cat households need one litter box per cat plus one extra to reduce territorial stress.
- Play aggression (biting, ambushing feet) is normal in kittens but should be redirected to toys to prevent escalating behavior in adulthood.
- Cats benefit greatly from vertical space (cat trees, shelves) — height gives them security and satisfies natural surveillance instincts.
