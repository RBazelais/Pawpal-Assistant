# Cat Health Basics — Minor Wellness & Home Care

- Plain pumpkin puree (not spiced pie filling) is a widely recommended dietary remedy for mild constipation or digestive upset in cats — 1–2 teaspoons mixed into food adds soluble fiber that supports healthy gut motility. (Source: Cornell Feline Health Center, veterinary nutrition guidelines)
- Cats occasionally vomit from eating too fast, hairballs, or minor dietary indiscretion — isolated incidents with normal behavior afterward are generally not an emergency.
- Hairball vomiting is normal in cats that groom frequently; regular brushing and hairball-formula food or treats can reduce frequency by lowering the amount of ingested fur.
- A bland diet of plain boiled chicken (no seasoning) is commonly recommended by vets as a short-term option when a cat has a mild upset stomach and is otherwise acting normally.
- Cats are prone to constipation, especially as they age; increased water intake (via wet food or a pet fountain) and regular exercise support healthy digestion.
- Persistent vomiting (more than 2–3 times in a day), vomiting blood, or vomiting combined with lethargy, hiding, or appetite loss requires a veterinarian visit.
- A cat that has not used the litter box for more than 48 hours, or that strains without producing output, should be seen by a vet — urinary blockages are a common and serious emergency in cats.
- Cats need fresh water at all times; dehydration worsens digestive issues — wet food provides roughly 70–80% moisture and is the most effective way to increase daily water intake.
- Probiotic supplements formulated for cats (not humans) may support gut health during or after dietary changes; always choose products specifically labeled for cats.
- Signs of minor digestive upset that can be monitored at home: one or two vomiting episodes, eating and drinking normally afterward, normal energy and no blood present.
