# Cat Grooming

- Cats self-groom extensively, but regular brushing is still important to reduce hairballs and prevent matting in medium/long-haired breeds.
- Short-haired cats should be brushed once a week; long-haired cats (Persian, Maine Coon, Ragdoll) need daily brushing to prevent painful mats.
- Cats rarely need baths because their saliva contains natural cleaning agents, but bathing may be needed after exposure to toxic substances or for certain skin conditions.
- Hairballs (cylindrical vomited masses of fur) are a normal byproduct of grooming; frequent hairballs can be reduced with regular brushing and hairball-formula food.
- Nail trimming every 2–3 weeks prevents overgrowth, curling into the paw pad, and reduces damage from scratching.
- The quick (pink blood vessel) inside the nail is visible in light-colored nails; trim just the clear, curved tip to avoid bleeding.
- Cat ears should be checked weekly; a small amount of light brown wax is normal, but dark crumbly debris may indicate ear mites.
- Teeth should be brushed with cat-safe toothpaste 2–3 times per week; dental disease affects over 70% of cats by age 3.
- Eye discharge is normal in small amounts; excessive, colored, or crusted discharge is a sign that warrants a vet check.
- A cat that suddenly stops grooming, or conversely over-grooms to the point of hair loss (psychogenic alopecia), is signaling a problem.
- Cats should not be shaved unless medically necessary — their coat regulates body temperature in both heat and cold.
- Senior cats (11+) may groom less effectively due to arthritis or dental pain; extra brushing helps compensate.
